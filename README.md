# Project 2 - Early vs. Delayed Childbearing and Socioeconomic Outcomes

## 📖 Overview
This project examines the relationship between age at first childbirth and current socioeconomic status among women in the United States. Using nationally representative data, the analysis explores how reproductive timing may influence long-term economic and health outcomes—an important topic in public health and health equity research.

## ❓ Research Question
Is age at first birth associated with current socioeconomic status among U.S. women?

## 📊 Data Source
**Data Source:** National Health and Nutrition Examination Survey (NHANES)  
**Population:** U.S. women  

**Key variables:**
- Age at first birth  
- Poverty Income Ratio (PIR)  
- Age  
- Race/Ethnicity  

## 🧪 Methods
The analysis was conducted in RStudio and included:

- Data cleaning and handling of missing values  
- Recoding variables for analysis  
- Descriptive statistics to summarize key characteristics  
- Data visualization (violin plots, boxplots, scatterplots)  
- Exploratory analysis to assess relationships between variables  

## 📊 Statistical Analysis
- Linear regression model to assess the relationship between age at first birth and Poverty Income Ratio  
- Logistic regression model to estimate the probability of being above or below the U.S. poverty threshold  
- Adjusted model controlling for potential confounders (e.g., age and race/ethnicity)  

## 📈 Key Findings
- Later age at first birth was associated with higher Poverty Income Ratios  
- Early childbearing was linked to lower socioeconomic outcomes  
- Disparities observed across groups suggest the influence of broader structural and social determinants of health

## ⚠️ Limitations
- The cross-sectional nature of NHANES data limits the ability to establish temporal relationships  
- Age at first birth is self-reported and may be subject to recall bias  
- Poverty Income Ratio reflects current status and may not capture life course changes  
- Residual confounding may remain due to unmeasured variables  

## 🌍 Significance
These findings contribute to a broader understanding of how reproductive timing intersects with socioeconomic outcomes. Using nationally representative NHANES data, this project highlights the role of social determinants of health and supports a life course perspective in public health research. The results may help inform policies and interventions aimed at improving economic stability and health outcomes among women.
